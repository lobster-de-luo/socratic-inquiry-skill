# Socratic Inquiry · Case Template: Abstract Topics

## Applicable Scenarios
- Philosophical questions (free will, consciousness, ethics)
- Value judgments (what is good life, justice)
- Abstract concepts (understanding, intelligence, creativity)
- Belief examination (why believe X, where are X's boundaries)

## Characteristics
- User usually has preliminary stance but untested
- Key terms require extensive clarification
- Assumptions often deeply hidden
- May have no "correct answer," but process has value

---

## [Stage 1: Clarification] Key Points

**Strategy**: The biggest trap in abstract topics is "everyone means something different."

**Common Openers**:
- "When you say 'X,' what specifically do you mean?"
- "Can you give a concrete example of what counts as X and what doesn't?"
- "What's the difference between this and 'Y' (similar concept)?"

**Stage Completion Signals**:
- Mutual agreement on definition of key terms
- Or explicit acknowledgment "we're discussing X at different levels"

---

## [Stage 2: Assumption Probing] Key Points

**Strategy**: Assumptions in abstract topics often involve worldviews.

**Common Follow-ups**:
- "You seem to assume X. Why do you believe this premise holds?"
- "Is this judgment based on experience, intuition, or some theoretical framework?"
- "If we change cultural/historical context, does this assumption still hold?"

**Stage Completion Signals**:
- Implicit meta-assumptions made explicit
- User acknowledges "this is indeed my premise, but I haven't tested it"

---

## [Stage 3: Reasoning & Evidence] Key Points

**Strategy**: "Evidence" in abstract topics is often analogy, thought experiments, authority views.

**Common Follow-ups**:
- "What is the reasoning chain for this conclusion? Is each step solid?"
- "Are there counterexamples or edge cases?"
- "If we accept your reasoning, what absurd conclusions follow? (Reductio ad absurdum)"

**Stage Completion Signals**:
- Weak points in reasoning chain identified
- Or argument confirmed as internally consistent

---

## [Stage 4: Viewpoint Shifting] Key Points

**Strategy**: Abstract topics most need viewpoint shifting—philosophy is "gymnastics of thought experiments."

**Common Follow-ups**:
- "How would someone holding the opposite view refute you?"
- "If we replace 'X' with 'Y' in what you said, does the reasoning still hold?"
- "From another discipline/culture/species perspective, does this question still make sense?"

**Stage Completion Signals**:
- User can restate or understand opposing position
- Or discover "this question itself may be biased"

---

## [Stage 5: Consequences] Key Points

**Strategy**: Consequences of abstract topics are often "if everyone thought/did this."

**Common Follow-ups**:
- "If society generally accepts this conclusion, what happens?"
- "In extreme cases (99% vs 1%), does your position remain stable?"
- "What practical guidance does this conclusion offer for concrete decisions?"

**Stage Completion Signals**:
- Practical consequences of position fully explored
- Or discover "this is a pure conceptual question with no practical consequences"

---

## [Stage 6: Meta-Questioning] Key Points

**Strategy**: Abstract topics most need this step—the question may be more important than the answer.

**Common Follow-ups**:
- "Why do you care about this question? What does it mean to you?"
- "Is this the right question to ask? Or is there a better way to frame it?"
- "Which matters more: exploring this question itself, or getting an answer?"

**Stage Completion Signals**:
- User has awareness of question's motivation and boundaries
- Or discover "this question may have no final answer"

## Practical Tips

1. **Stay Humble**: Abstract topics have no standard answers; inquiry aims at clarification, not "winning"
2. **Allow Suspension**: Sometimes the best conclusion is "this question needs more information/better framework"
3. **Watch Emotions**: Philosophical questions often touch core values; watch for user's emotional signals
4. **Beware Term Traps**: Be wary of "big words" (e.g., "essence," "true," "ultimate")—they're often sources of vagueness
5. **Timely Closure**: Abstract topics can go infinitely deep; watch the 30-round limit and summarize promptly
